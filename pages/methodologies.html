🔄 Development Methodologies

Agile, Waterfall, RAD & LEAN

Compare the major software development methodologies, know when to use each, and understand how to justify your choice in the OSP proposal — a frequent Distinction differentiator.

🎯
Exam relevance: The mark scheme expects you to justify your development approach in your proposal and demonstrate it throughout your documentation. Distinction requires you to reference your chosen methodology and explain why it suits the project context — not just name it.

🏃 Agile

Agile Methodology

Iterative, incremental development with continuous feedback and adaptation

Most Common

Agile is a family of methodologies based on the Agile Manifesto (2001), which prioritises: individuals and interactions over processes; working software over documentation; customer collaboration over contract negotiation; responding to change over following a plan.

Work is broken into short cycles called sprints (typically 1–4 weeks). At the end of each sprint, a working increment is delivered and reviewed. Requirements can evolve — making Agile ideal for projects where needs aren't fully known upfront.

✅ Advantages

  • Responds quickly to changing requirements
  • Regular delivery of working software
  • Continuous stakeholder involvement reduces risk of wrong product
  • Early detection of problems through frequent testing
  • Suits the OSP iterative development requirement directly

❌ Disadvantages

  • Hard to predict final cost and timeline upfront
  • Requires high client involvement — not always available
  • Can lead to scope creep without clear boundaries
  • Less suitable when requirements are completely fixed
  • Documentation often lighter — may conflict with regulated industries
Best for: Projects with evolving requirements, startups, web/app development, innovation projects, the OSP itself (iterative development is explicitly required by the mark scheme).

Agile Key Terms for Exams

TermDefinition
SprintA fixed-length iteration (1–4 weeks) producing a potentially shippable increment

💡
Scrum vs Agile: Scrum is a specific framework for implementing Agile. Agile is the philosophy; Scrum is one way to do it (with defined roles: Product Owner, Scrum Master, Development Team). Kanban is another Agile framework focused on visualising and limiting work-in-progress.

📈 Scaled Agile (SAFe)

Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe)

Agile practices applied across multiple teams in large organisations

Enterprise

Agile works well for a single team, but large organisations with 50–5000+ developers need a way to coordinate multiple Agile teams working on the same product. SAFe provides structure for this through three levels:

👤

Team Level

Individual Scrum teams run 2-week sprints. Each team has a Product Owner and Scrum Master.

🏢

Programme Level

5–12 Agile teams form an Agile Release Train (ART), synchronised on a common PI (Programme Increment) of 8–12 weeks.

🏭

Portfolio Level

Strategic themes align multiple ARTs with business goals. Budgets and roadmaps managed at this level.

Used by: Large enterprises — Spotify, NHS Digital, major banks. Not relevant for solo/small projects but important to know for your wider context section on current industry practice.

💧 Waterfall

Waterfall Methodology

Sequential, phase-by-phase development — each phase must complete before the next begins

Traditional

Waterfall follows a rigid linear sequence. Requirements are fully defined upfront, then each phase flows downward (like a waterfall) — there is no going back to a previous phase without significant cost.

✅ Advantages

  • Clear structure — easy to plan, budget and manage
  • Thorough documentation at every stage
  • Works well when requirements are stable and fully known
  • Easier for regulatory/compliance projects (healthcare, aviation)
  • Clear milestones and deliverables

❌ Disadvantages

  • Very expensive to change requirements mid-project
  • Working software not delivered until late in the process
  • Customer doesn't see product until near the end — high risk of wrong product
  • Testing happens late — defects found late are costly to fix
  • Doesn't suit modern fast-moving tech projects
Best for: Fixed-scope government contracts, safety-critical systems (aircraft, medical devices), projects where requirements genuinely cannot change (embedded systems, construction software).

⚡ Rapid Application Development (RAD)

Rapid Application Development

Speed-focused iterative development using prototyping and user feedback loops

Fast Delivery

RAD, developed by James Martin in 1991, prioritises speed of delivery over exhaustive planning. It uses prototyping extensively — building rough versions quickly, showing them to users, gathering feedback, and refining. The aim is to deliver a working product in weeks rather than months.

📋

Requirements Planning

High-level workshop with stakeholders. Enough to start — not exhaustive specification.

🖼️

User Design

Rapid prototyping with continuous user feedback. Users are heavily involved throughout.

🏗️

Construction

Developers and users work together to build and refine. Automated code generation tools used where possible.

✅ Advantages

  • Very fast time-to-working-product
  • High user involvement reduces misalignment risk
  • Flexible — easy to change direction based on feedback
  • Well-suited to UI-heavy projects

❌ Disadvantages

  • Requires skilled developers — speed demands expertise
  • Can sacrifice architecture quality for speed
  • Less suitable for large teams or complex backend systems
  • Risk of "prototype becoming product" with technical debt
Best for: Small–medium projects with engaged users, UI/UX-focused apps, proof-of-concept projects, startups needing quick MVPs (Minimum Viable Products).

♻️ LEAN Software Development

LEAN Development

Eliminate waste, amplify learning, deliver fast — adapted from Toyota Production System

7 Principles

LEAN originates from Toyota's manufacturing philosophy and was adapted for software by Mary and Tom Poppendieck. The core idea: maximise value by eliminating everything that doesn't directly contribute to the customer's needs (waste).

The 7 LEAN Principles

#PrincipleWhat it means in software
1Eliminate WasteRemove anything not adding value: unnecessary features, waiting time, defects, excess process
2Amplify LearningShort feedback cycles, code reviews, pair programming, retrospectives
3Decide as Late as PossibleDelay irreversible decisions until you have more information (keeps options open)
4Deliver as Fast as PossibleSmall batches, continuous deployment, reduce handoff delays
5Empower the TeamDecisions made by those closest to the work — not just management
6Build Integrity InQuality built into the process — not tested in at the end
7See the WholeOptimise the whole system, not just individual components
LEAN vs Agile: Agile focuses on how to develop (sprints, ceremonies); LEAN focuses on what to eliminate (waste reduction). Many modern teams combine both — Kanban is a LEAN-inspired Agile framework.

⭐ Distinction-Level Justification

🟡 Pass

I will use Agile because it is flexible and allows me to change things as I go. It is better than Waterfall because Waterfall is too rigid.

🟣 Distinction

I selected an Agile/Scrum-inspired methodology for the Resource Hub project because requirements were not fully defined at the outset — the client (the college) would need to review early prototypes before confirming final feature priorities (e.g. whether admin approval should be automatic or manual). Agile's iterative sprint structure maps directly onto the OSP's required iterative development lifecycle (Requirements → Design → Implementation → Review).

Waterfall was rejected because it requires fully-specified requirements upfront — inappropriate for a project where user feedback would inevitably refine the booking conflict logic and UI layout. RAD was considered but discarded because, while speed is valuable, the authorisation and security requirements (RBAC, CSRF protection) demanded careful architectural planning rather than rapid prototyping that might introduce technical debt.

LEAN principles were applied within the Agile framework to eliminate waste: I did not build features not requested by the client (YAGNI — You Aren't Gonna Need It), and kept documentation proportional to its value rather than comprehensive for its own sake.
